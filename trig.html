Triangles aren't just mathematically significant, they are also fundamental to the way we build our environments, both physical and virtual. Triangles are special because they are exceptionally strong.

Understand Triangles

  • A triangle is a three-sided polygon. The three sides are connected with each other end to end at a point, which forms the angles of the triangle. The sum of all three angles of the triangle is equal to 180 degrees. Understanding triangles is very important in both Geometry and Trigonometry.

Classification of triangles

  • Triangles can be classified by either their angles or sides.

Classification with angles

  • An acute triangle is a triangle with all angles less than 90°.
  • The right triangle is a triangle with 2 angles less than 90° and one angle equal to 90°.
  • An obtuse triangle is a triangle with 2 angles less than 90° and one angle greater 90°.

Classification with sides

  • An equilateral triangle is a triangle with all three sides equal to one another.
  • An Isosceles triangle is a triangle with only two of the three sides equal to each other.
  • A scalene triangle is a triangle with none of the three sides equal to one another.

In addition,

  • The acute triangle can be Scalene, Isosceles, or Equilateral. But, obtuse and right triangles can either be Scalene or Isosceles, never Equilateral.

Last but definitely not least

  • The sum of any given triangle angles always adds up to 180°.

Trig ratios (SOH-CAH-TOA)

There are six trigonometric ratios, Sine (Sin), Cosine (Cos), Tangent (Tan), Cotangent(Cot), Cosecant (Cosec), and Secant (Sec). However, for this section we going to focus on the three common ones (Sin, Cos, and Tan)

Name of each side of a right-angle triangle.

  • Hypotenuse - The longer side of the right-angle triangle.
  • Opposite - The side opposite the angle.
  • Adjacent - the non-hypotenuse side that is next to a given angle

Trig ratios

  • Sin (Angle A) = Opposite / Hypotenuse (SOH)
  • Cos (Angle A) = Adjecent / Hypotenuse (CAH)
  • Tan (Angle A) = Opposite / Adjacent (TOA)

SOH-CAH-TOA is used when the triangle is a right-angle triangle, the angle other than the right-angle is given, and a length of one side of that triangle .

Trig ratio reciprocals

Reciprocals of basic trigonometric ratios are the inverse values of the sin, cos, and tan values that are computed by reciprocating the sides required for computing the ratio.

Was this lesson helpful?

thumb_up thumb_down
×

Trig ratio reciprocals

These include three extra ratios that are the reciprocals of the three basic trig ratios.

  • Cosecant (Csc) is the reciprocal of sin. Therefore, Csc = 1/Sinθ
    Csc = 1/Opposite/Hypotenuse
    Use the rules for dividing fractions, which is to change the division sign to multiplication sign and flip the second fraction.
    Csc = 1/1 * Hypotenuse / Opposite
    everything multiplied by is itself
    ∴ Csc = Hypotenuse / Opposite
  • Secant (Sec) is the reciprocal of cos. Therefore, Sec = 1/Cosθ
    Sec = 1/Adjacent/Hypotenuse
    Use the rules for dividing fractions, which is to change the division sign to multiplication sign and flip the second fraction.
    Sec = 1/1 * Hypotenuse / Adjacent
    everything multiplied by is itself
    ∴ Sec = Hypotenuse / Adjacent
  • Cotangent (Cot) is the reciprocal of tan. Therefore, Cot = 1/Tanθ
    Cot = 1/Opposite/Adjacent
    Use the rules for dividing fractions, which is to change the division sign to multiplication sign and flip the second fraction.
    Cot = 1/1 * Adjacent / Opposite
    everything multiplied by is itself
    ∴ Cot = Adjacent / Opposite

Area of a triangle

    Right-angle triangle
  • Finding the area of a right angle triangle is fairly easy, you use the equaton: Area = 1/2*Base*height
  • Area = 1/2*9m*4m
    Area = 18 m^2
    • Non-right triangles
  • The equation is: Area = 1/2*a*bSin(C)
  • To find the area of a triangle, you need base and height. in non-right angle traingles, the height is usually unknown. Therefore, you have to find it using Trig ratios
  • Given angle = C.
    a = base
    b = hypotenuse
    Height = Opposite
    Sin (C) = Height/ b
    Height = b*Sin(C)
    Therefore, Area = 1/2*a*bSin(C)
    E.g.,
    Area = 1/2* 13 cm * 8cm * Sin(42)
    Area = 35 cm^2.

Understanding trig functions using the unit circle

  • In trig functions, cosθ is defined as a x-coordinate and sinθ as a y-coordinate of a point on the unit circle with an angle θ between positive x-axis and the radius to your point.
  • Cosθ = adj/hyp
    Cosθ = x/1
    Cosθ = x
  • Sinθ = opp/hyp
    Sinθ = = y/1
    sinθ = y;
  • Tanθ = opp/adj
    Tanθ = y/x
    tanθ = Sinθ/Cosθ

Pythagoras Theorem on the unit circle

  • a2 + b2 = c2
    Where:
    a = adj
    ∴ on the unit circle, a = x = cosθ
    b = opp
    ∴ on the unit circle, b = y = cosθ
    c = hyp
    ∴ on the unit circle, c = 1. Therefore, tanθ = Sinθ/Cosθ = 1, meaning tanθ in trig functions is equal to 1.
  • This therefore, tells us that the pythagoras theorem in trig functions can be written as:
    Cos2θ + Sin2θ = tan2θ
    Knowing the tanθ = 1
    We can subsiquently write the equation as:
    Cos2θ + Sin2θ = 12
    Since 1 multiplied by 1 is 1, the final equation is therefore
    Cos2θ + Sin2θ = 1
    Adiitionally, this means Cos2θ + Sin2θ = Sin2θ/Cos2θ.
